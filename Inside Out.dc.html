Concept

The design concept is rooted in the notion that people experience and perceive space and the environment through their sensory faculties and physical bodies, which gradually shape their everyday existence. However, we often encounter unexpected and extraordinary occurrences that intersect with our ordinary lives, such as the warmth of sunlight on our skin, the sight of children playing freely in a playground, the sound of raindrops pattering on the rooftop, or the fluttering of swallows' wings. Considering this, the proposal aims to design a building that is connected to the lives of local residents, considers the surrounding environment and its function, and reflects elements of life and rituals in food culture through multisensory experiences in daily life.

The primary target of the proposed building is the students at the nearby hospitality school and residents. The process of making Chinese pastries is sequentially broken down into distinct architectural spaces that offer multisensory experiences, ranging from ingredient selection, dough and filling preparation, dough rolling and filling wrapping, pastry mold stamping and coloring, baking, cooling, and packaging. The physical movements and sensory experiences associated with each step are classified into corresponding "intense" sensory experiences, and the spatial form is shaped by the physical movements of making pastries, with the overall flow line connected based on site conditions.

The experience sequence of spatial script arrangement emphasizes body posture, visual light and shadow, hearing, smell, and framing. In contrast to spiritual life ceremonies, the proposal aims to raise awareness of one's "present self-existence" through the experience of making pastries.

The building's primary function is a Chinese pastry workshop, where the entire pastry-making process relies on people's focus on their movements and senses, such as the aroma of ingredients, the sound of pounding molds, and the force of rolling dough. Through architectural and material techniques, the present experiences and emotions are "enhanced," creating the anticipated experiential scene in each space, with the expectation that the public will leave behind "a moment of good life memory" during the process.
